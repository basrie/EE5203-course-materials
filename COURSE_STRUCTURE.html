EE5203 Microprocessors - 2026-2027 Course Structure

Status: design baseline for iteration. Lecture 1 is the reference package; later lecture materials will be revised after that package is approved.

Course Design Statement

EE5203 develops embedded-system competence through a C-first STM32 pathway. Students first build reliable Embedded C foundations, then learn enough register-level programming to understand the machine, and finally use STM32 HAL to implement increasingly complex peripherals and integrated systems.

The course follows four principles:

  1. Embedded C receives four full lectures. Later material assumes demonstrated competence in functions, arrays, bitwise operations, pointers, structures, memory, and volatile.
  2. Register literacy, HAL fluency. GPIO and simple register operations are implemented directly. Complex peripherals are implemented with HAL while students inspect and interpret the important registers.
  3. Laboratories fade their scaffolding. Early labs teach the workflow; later labs provide specifications, references, tests, and individual checkoffs.
  4. The project starts early and grows through milestones. Students see the destination in L01, form teams in L05, and demonstrate working subsystems throughout the term.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, a successful student can:

  1. Write, compile, debug, and test Embedded C programs.
  2. Explain the Cortex-M memory, register, and exception model.
  3. Configure GPIO, timers, PWM, ADC, UART, and a synchronous serial peripheral using an appropriate combination of CMSIS register access and STM32 HAL.
  4. Design interrupt-driven, non-blocking embedded applications.
  5. Interpret datasheets, reference manuals, timing diagrams, compiler messages, debugger state, and peripheral registers as engineering evidence.
  6. Integrate, test, document, demonstrate, and individually explain an STM32-based embedded system.

Core Material Package

Each lecture has one landing-page section with:

  1. Prepare: objectives, short preparation task, and pre-lab quiz.
  2. Learn: core theory deck and two-page study sheet.
  3. Practise: 30-45 minute worksheet and delayed worked solutions.
  4. Lab: two-page lab card, reference page, starter files when justified, checkoff requirements, and submission link.
  5. Review: mastery checklist and 5-8 exam-style questions.

Material limits:

  • Core theory deck: target 28-32 slides; hard maximum 35.
  • Laboratory opening deck/briefing: maximum 12-15 slides when slides are needed.
  • Study sheet: maximum two pages.
  • Student lab card: maximum two pages and normally 700-900 words.
  • Practice worksheet: 30-45 minutes of work.
  • Pre-lab quiz: five questions, two attempts, highest score recorded.
  • Complete solutions remain under private/solutions/ until their release time.

Instructor-side, each lecture also carries an instructor guide and a one-page TA prep sheet (private/instructor/L##/TA_Prep_L##.md): prep checklist (the TA does the lab on hardware before the session), expected-evidence quick reference, a symptom-to-first-question triage table, rubric summary, oral spot-checks, and boundaries (what not to reveal). General TA policy — the evidence-first triage method, checkoff etiquette, solutions-access rules — lives once in private/instructor/TA_HANDBOOK.md; prep sheets stay lecture-specific.

Exit Tickets and In-Lecture Pulse Checks (Google Forms)

Every theory deck ends with an exit ticket delivered as a Google Forms quiz reached by a QR code on the closing slide (3 questions from the deck’s exit question, plus a required student-number field). Standard settings, proven with the L02 template form:

  • Quiz mode; release grades immediately; show missed questions and points but hide correct answers (limits leakage from early finishers’ screens). The key is revealed in the next lecture’s retrieval segment.
  • Collect verified email addresses (Google sign-in required) and limit to one response per account. Students sign in with personal Gmail, so the student number is the roster key; the verified email links one person’s tickets across weeks.
  • Ungraded: the tickets inform teaching (which misconception is alive in the room) and feed the retrieval slides — they never enter the grade workbook.
  • Production: copy the previous lecture’s form in Drive (settings and structure carry over), swap the questions, publish, regenerate the QR (segno one-liner into lectures/L##_*/assets/exit_ticket_qr.svg). Form URLs are logged per lecture in MATERIALS_STATUS.md.
  • Graded pre-lab quizzes stay in CATS Tests & Quizzes, not Google Forms — anything touching the Laboratory grade needs the official LMS record and the two-attempt policy.

One core slide normally serves one instructional purpose. Full programs, alternative implementations, long setup sequences, detailed peripheral tables, bonus topics, and complete solutions belong in references, source files, or appendices rather than the planned teaching sequence.

Register/HAL Boundary

  • Use CMSIS symbolic register structures, not repeated raw numeric addresses.
  • Implement RCC enable and GPIO fundamentals directly at register level.
  • Use HAL for interrupts, timers, PWM, input capture, ADC, UART, I2C/SPI, and advanced integration after explaining the hardware model.
  • Inspect important registers after HAL configuration.
  • Declare the ownership model of every exercise: bare metal, HAL, or one controlled post-HAL register modification.
  • Keep LL as an optional industry reference rather than a third core dialect.

Iteration Rule

L01 is the reference implementation for navigation, document length, visual hierarchy, preparation, practice, lab checkoffs, and instructor support. Revise L01 until approved before mechanically applying its pattern to L02-L14.
